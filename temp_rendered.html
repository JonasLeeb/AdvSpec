Lecture 2 Adv Spectroscopy IR and Raman Spectroscopy

Molecular Vibrations

Fundamentals

  • Can be modeled as harmonic oscillators (spring-mass system)
  • Frequency depends on bond strength and atomic masses
    • \(\nu = \frac{1}{2\pi}{\sqrt{ \frac{{k(m_{1}+m_{2})}}{m_{1}\cdot m_{2}} }}\)
  • Two complementary techniques to probe this mode: IR absorption and Raman scattering

IR vs Raman Selection Rules

  • IR: Requires change in electric dipole moment during vibration
    • light needs to couple to the vibration, this only works if there is a change in dipole moment
    • \(C=C\) stretch works since double (and triple) bonds are easily polarizable
    • symmetric molecules like \(CO_2\) don't work
  • Raman: Requires polarizability to change during the vibration
    • Raman distorts the electronic cloud, if the cloud is easily distorted and this changes the polarizability (symmetric distortion often don't change polarizability) one gets a strong Raman signal
    • Polarizablility is affected by: size of atoms (larger is better), conjugated systems (Delocalized electrons is good), symmetric molecules (often highly polarizable), many identical bonds
  • For more see [[Notes on selection rules for Raman and IR (AI)]]

Raman scattering mechanism

  • most scattering is elastic (Rayleigh), so no change in energy
  • much rarer: part of the photon energy is transferred into a vibrational mode of the molecule
  • difference in energy corresponds to a vibrational quantum
  • Stokes: electron is in the ground state before excitation
  • Anti-Stokes: electron is in a vibrational state -> Raman scattered photon can get higher energy than before scattering (much rarer, since dependent on vibrational level it is bolzmann distributed)
    • Ratio of anti stokes vs stokes \(\frac{N_{1}}{N_{0}}=\exp\left( -\frac{\Delta E}{kT} \right)\), \(kT \approx 200 cm^{-1}\) at room T (or 210 cm\(^{-1}\) at 300K)
    • If Temperature is not room temp use formula from [[Lecture 1 Adv Spectroscopy Fluorescence]], but watch out for the units (cm\(^{-1}\))
  • Usually we measure the shift from the original energy of the photon (Raman shift), so that the original energy doesn't matter
  • Raman intensity: \(I_{R} = k \cdot l_{0} \cdot \alpha \cdot v_{4}\)
    • \(I_{0}\) is the laser power, which depends on the absorption and photodamage
    • \(\alpha\) is the polarizability (molecular property), which can be influenced in RRS
    • \(k\) is the instrument factor. This must be optimized
    • \(v^4\) is an effect that increases the Raman signal dramatically for shorter excitation wavelengths (longer wavelengths are more commonly used due to less fluorescence interference)

Applications of Raman Spectroscopy

  • Art authentication (Fast, simple, non invasive)
  • Identification of microplastics
  • Cancer diagnosis (small differences in lipid/protein ratios)

Practical Advantages and Limitations

Strengths

  • Boadly applicable
  • Possible to measure in-situ
  • Non-contact, non-destructive
  • Molecular identification (also gives information about the environment, crystal form, binding, etc)
  • Little or no sample prep required
  • Little interference from water
  • Submicrometer spatial resolution

Weaknesses

  • Inherently weak signal
  • Spectral resolution (line width) limited by bandwidth of excitation source
  • Poor sensitivity under dilute conditions (Raman signal dominated by solvent/matrix)
  • For low energy excitation (NIR) the resulting Raman photons have very low energy and are hard to detect, especially for higher vibrational states ![[Pasted image 20250611153136.png|200]]

Setups

  • Laser purity is essential, non pure laser will cause stray peaks
  • Often backscattering setup (90 degree or not) setup
  • Transmission setup for less signal from surface
  • Fiber optic setups also possible (6 fibers for collection around one fiber for excitation)
    • Fiber optics have problem of also causing Raman signal:
      • use Bandpass filter at the end of excitation fiber to filter out Raman from fiber
      • use long pass filter at entrance of collection fiber to filter our reflected laser light

Special modes of Raman

Resonance Raman Spectroscopy (RRS)

Concept:

  • Use excitation wavelength close to actual excited state (vibrational state of \(S_1\)) --> Raman signals are orders of magnitude stronger
  • \(\alpha \approx \frac{1}{\omega_{Gl} - \omega_{L}- i T_{l}}\)
  • Denominator becomes very small near excited states
  • Only transitions that match the geometry change in the excited state get enhanced

Result:

Signal is enhanced by several orders of magnitude, but only for the chromophore (the part of a molecule that absorbs vis/UV light)

Problem:

  • Strong fluorescence interference often stronger than Raman signal
  • Loss of information, since only chromophore bands are enhanced

Options to reduce fluorescence

  • Shifted excitation wavelength
  • Adding quenchers (e.g. \(O_2\))
  • Deep-UV excitation < 260 nm
  • Time-gated detection

Time-Resolved Raman

Fluorescence suppression

  • Excitation: ps pulsed laser

  • Raman process is instantaneous -> faster than fluorescence

  • Gated detector closes before most of the fluorescence signal arrives (closing achieved using an Intensifier than can be switched off (Multichannel plate))

  • Achieves 10-100x fluorescence suppression

Problem: For large samples not all the Raman signal arrives fast enough

Subsurface Analysis (TRRS)

  • Raman photons from deeper layers arrive at the detector at a later time due to scattering
  • Different time windows select different depths
  • Effective photon speed 20x slower than in scattering media
  • Applications: explosives detection through containers, astrobiology (looking inside stones)
