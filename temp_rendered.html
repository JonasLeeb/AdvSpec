Solvatochrmoism (Polarity Probe)

Electron distribution becomes more polarized in excited state

  • Solvent molecules reorientate to stabilize this new electron distribution
  • The more polar the solvent the more the excited state is stabilized
  • stabilization means the excited states energy is lowered -> emission to ground state wavelength increases -> ground state energy is also raised slightly (becuase the solvent molecules have reoriented, so that the excited state is more energetically favorable)
  • Depends on how quickly the solvent molecules can reorientate -> For high Viscosity the excited state cannot be fully stabilized before the molecule emits -> Shorter wavelength than for lower viscosity and same polarity of solvent
  • can be used to classify micro plastics
    • Nile Red is used to as a fluorescent dye
    • NR adsorbs to the surface of polymers but not other stuff in the solution
    • surface polarity is different between differnt types of plastics, which results in a different emission wavelength of the fluorescence of NR

pH sensitivity

  • different properties for protonated (acidic) and deprotonated (basic) forms. Ratio depedns on local pH and probes pK\(_a\) (pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA]))
  • Intensity based pH probes:
    • acidic and basic forms of fluorophore have different quantum yields (\(\phi\))
    • if one form is highly fluorescent, the total emission will tell us about changes in pH
    • Disadvantages:
      • Probe concentration must be well known
      • Inner filter effects
      • Photodegradation affects measurements
      • Instrument variations
  • Ratiometric pH probes
    • acidic and basic forms have different emission wavelengths -> Measure fluorescence at two different wavelengths and look at ratio -> Eliminates concetration dependence
    • Example: Excited-State Intramolecular Proton Transfer (ESIPT)
      • In the excited state the molecule transfers a proton from one site to another, changing its emission properties, this depends on ph and polarity
      • Back proton transfer (BPT) in ground state
